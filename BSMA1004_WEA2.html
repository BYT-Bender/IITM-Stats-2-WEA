Description

In this activity, I performed a Monte Carlo simulation to find the probability of each face (1 through 6) appearing when a fair six-sided die is rolled. The goal was to compare the theoretical probability with the experimental probability obtained from the simulation.

I conducted the simulation for three different scenarios: rolling the die 10 times, 50 times, and 100 times.

  • Theoretical Probability: For a fair die, the probability of any single face appearing is 1/6, which is approximately 0.167 or 16.67%. This value is constant and based on mathematical theory.
  • Monte Carlo Simulation: I used Python to simulate the die rolls, counted the occurrences of each face, and calculated the experimental probability for each case (10, 50, and 100 rolls).

The results showed that as the number of rolls increased, the experimental probabilities from the simulation tended to get closer to the theoretical probability, demonstrating a fundamental principle of statistics.

Analysis and Conclusion

The simulation results from rolling the die 10, 50, and 100 times clearly demonstrate the Law of Large Numbers. By comparing the Monte Carlo probabilities from each experiment to the constant theoretical probability of approximately 0.17 (or 1/6), we can draw the following conclusions:

For 10 Throws: The results are highly varied and not close to the theoretical probability. For example, faces '1' and '2' appeared with a probability of 0.30, which is almost double the expected value. This small sample size leads to significant random fluctuation.

For 50 Throws: The probabilities are beginning to stabilize and get closer to the theoretical value. The highest probability was 0.20 (for faces '2' and '4') and the lowest was 0.14 (for faces '1', '5', and '6'). The spread is much smaller compared to the 10-throw simulation.

For 100 Throws: These results are, on average, the closest to the theoretical probability of 0.17. While there is still some randomness, such as face '2' appearing with a probability of 0.22, most of the values are clustering more tightly around the expected value.

Conclusion: This activity confirms that while Monte Carlo probabilities can be unpredictable with a small number of trials, they become more accurate and converge toward the theoretical probability as the number of trials increases.

Visualizing the Law of Large Numbers

The graph above clearly illustrates the core concept of this activity.

  • The blue line (Monte Carlo Simulation) is very unstable when the number of throws is low (on the left side of the graph), showing significant random fluctuations.
  • As the number of throws increases (moving to the right), the blue line stabilizes and gets progressively closer to the dashed red line (Theoretical Probability).

This visualization provides powerful evidence that the accuracy of a Monte Carlo simulation improves with a larger number of trials, perfectly confirming the Law of Large Numbers.
